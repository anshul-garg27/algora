# Q: Why are you leaving your current company?

> **LP**: Intro & Closers
> **Primary story**: W12 — Career trajectory, not Walmart-bashing
> **Backup story**: None — this is a positioning answer
> **Time budget**: 45–60 seconds spoken

---

## The spoken answer

Short version — I'm not running from Walmart, I'm running toward an Amazon-scale platform problem.

Walmart has been good for me. SE-III, 18 months in, real ownership of the audit-logging platform, the Spring Boot 3 migration, the supplier self-service work. I've had the autonomy to ship things end-to-end.

But two things are pulling me to Amazon at SDE-3.

First, **scope**. At Walmart Data Ventures, our biggest customers are a couple of hundred suppliers — Pepsi, Coca-Cola, that tier. Important, but bounded. The systems I'd build at Amazon SDE-3 are consumed by Amazon-internal teams at a scale where every design decision propagates. The audit-logging library I built reached three teams at Walmart. At Amazon, that same library shape reaches dozens. That's the leverage I want next.

Second, **platform DNA**. Walmart has good engineers, but it's a retail company that runs engineering. Amazon is an engineering company that happens to run retail and AWS. The default expectation at Amazon — own the service end-to-end, write the doc, defend the design — that's how I already work. I'd rather be in a place where that's the floor, not the ceiling.

I want to be clear — I'm not exiting Walmart because something broke. I'm choosing Amazon because the trajectory is right.

---

## Why this works

- **Forward-framed**, not backward. The "why I'm leaving" question is a trap — the answer should sound like "why I'm joining".
- **Two concrete reasons** — scope and platform DNA. Not "growth" or "challenge" (those are filler words).
- **Honest about Walmart** — you don't trash it. You name what's good ("I've had autonomy") before naming the gap.
- **Explicit closing line** — "I'm not exiting because something broke" — removes any suspicion of being managed out.

---

## Technical depth — if they probe

- **Scope evidence at Walmart**: the audit-log shared library was built for the Data Ventures org. Three teams adopted it in month one. The reach is bounded by Data Ventures' surface area — maybe 15 services org-wide. At Amazon, the same library shape lives inside hundreds of two-pizza teams.
- **Platform DNA evidence**: Walmart still ships with shared monorepos and team-by-team Confluence docs. Amazon's service-team model with internal docs and ownership boundaries is closer to how I already think.
- **What I'd bring**: the audit-logging architecture, the Spring Boot 3 migration discipline (Flagger canary 10→25→50→100, zero customer impact), and the supplier self-service BigQuery-RLS pattern. All translate cleanly to AWS / Amazon-internal teams.

## Likely follow-ups

**Q: Have you talked to your manager about this?**
> Yes. He knows I'm exploring. He's been supportive — he understands the SDE-3 jump at Amazon is the right shape for me. I'd rather have that conversation honestly than disappear.

**Q: What would Walmart need to do to keep you?**
> Honestly, this isn't a counter-offer conversation. The pull is structural — Amazon's scope at SDE-3 is something Walmart can't easily replicate in my current team without a relocation or org change I don't want. So no, I'm not negotiating, I'm moving.

**Q: Is compensation a factor?**
> Some, but not the headline. If comp were the only thing, I'd be looking at HFT or pre-IPO startups. I'm here for the scope and the engineering culture. Comp needs to be fair — that's table stakes.

**Q: What if Walmart shifts you to a bigger team?**
> If that had been on the table six months ago, the calculation might've been different. Right now my decision is made — I want the Amazon move.

**Q: Did the recent layoffs at Walmart affect this?**
> They weren't the trigger. I started exploring before the latest round. Layoffs are a reminder that career compounding only happens if you keep choosing the right next move — they didn't make my decision, but they confirmed the timing.

---

## What NOT to say

- **Never trash Walmart.** Not the manager, not the tech stack, not the culture. Interviewers assume you'll do the same to them in 18 months.
- Don't say "I'm bored" — that signals you've stopped finding leverage, not that the role is too small.
- Don't say "the layoffs" as the primary reason — even if it's a real factor. Frame the pull, not the push.
- Don't say "Walmart has bad engineering" — it doesn't, and the person across the table probably knows that.
- Don't promise to never leave Amazon — they don't believe it and you shouldn't say it.

---

## Spoken-vs-written delivery note

- Open with the headline: "Short version — I'm not running from Walmart, I'm running toward..." Sets the frame.
- The two reasons (scope + platform DNA) should sound like reasoning, not bullet points. Use connective tissue: "First..." then "Second..." with a beat in between.
- Slow down on "I'm not exiting Walmart because something broke." That's the line that defuses the recruiter's risk concern. Don't rush it.
- 45–60 seconds. Anything past 75 starts sounding defensive.
